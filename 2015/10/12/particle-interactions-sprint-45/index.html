Particle Interactions: Sprint 45 with William Hart

It was a nice chat with Will Hart, the leader of the Electron team at Particle. Will spoke with us last time about his experiences running supply chain from China, and he had some fun insights this time about the Electron, the new office, and some Particle powered Halloween hacks.

Give us an update on the Electron.

We are currently running an Electron beta. It's been a good opportunity to work on the firmware. The beta is worldwide, which has been particularly satisfying.

It's a very exciting time for the team.

And overall, it's going very well.

What's up with your hat, Will?

Will is wearing an M2M2U hat, which is a reference to the Electron Kickstarter video.

This was even an extremely stretchy stretch goal, but we didn't make it there.

What has been challenging in Electron development?

Connected devices can be complicated, but cellular makes for a particularly full stack.

Cellular connection behavior is one focus.

We don't have cellular towers-- we're using the towers of a carrier. That means that we need to be aware of rate limiting in a way that wasn't as much of a concern when carriers weren't involved.

Of course, we don't think people will spam the towers on purpose. But mistakes do happen during development. When people do this on Wi-Fi devices, we respond with rate limiting.

On the Core and the Photon, it even had a special name.

But carriers might not respond that way to spamming-- they might just ban us. So we're being very careful to develop safeguards for our users.

Tell us about the new office.

Particle just moved to an office in The Dogpatch. The office is shared with Prynt.

It's got more room, and we have a fancy conference table.

What else is challenging in Electron production?

Packaging is fun but surprisingly difficult.

That's partially because of some of the things that need to be included in the Electron bundle.

However, it's one of the most fun parts of the manufacturing process.

Jokes are pretty great.

Will we be able to move Photon code directly to the Electron?

Yes! On the Photon, we created a Hardware Abstraction Layer (HAL) that makes the code you write less dependent on the hardware you use. This means that your Photon code should be able to run on the Electron with few changes.

Does Particle.function() now work in the IDE?

Both Particle. and Spark. are currently options for functions, publishes, subscribes, and variables in the IDE.

What kind of cool things will people make with the Electron?

The Electron allows for accessibility of data-- it's now easy to get information, and there's a lot of possibility there.

In some ways, the Electron is to connected devices as the GoPro is to cameras.

Since it's relatively cheap at $39 for 2G, you can throw it out of a plane, float it down a river, or see how high it will soar on a weather balloon, without fear of losing out on a large investment.

Continuing the GoPro analogy, we're generally trying to build tools that make a beginner feel like a pro.

In the same way that you can put a camera on the front of your surfboard and feel like a pro, you can take the Photon or Electron out of the box, connect it, and feel like a pro product designer or hacker.

That fast onboarding experience is particularly interesting for the Electron, which could actually skip the entire Wi-Fi credentials setup and get you started much faster.

Let's talk Halloween.

According to Will, the number one use of Particle devices is Halloween hacks.

VP of Marketing Stephanie Rich once made a motion-detecting pumpkin.

Avidan Ross dressed up as the devil one year, and decked out his costume with Particle powered lights.

The Particle Collider may be the name of our slack group, but it was originally joked about as a name for a Particle powered dating app. What would that even look like?

Well, it's all about swiping left or right these days. What if you could wear a glove or finger stylus that also took your heart rate?

What's next for Will?

Back to China...

And that was the end!
